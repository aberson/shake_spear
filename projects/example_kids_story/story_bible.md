# Story Bible

## One-sentence premise

Nova runs the only bakery on the Moon, and the night before the Star Festival
she discovers the moon-sugar shipment is late — so she and her rescue-robot
Crumb must find another way to make the festival buns sweet.

## What kind of story is this?

A warm, funny picture-book adventure for ages 4-8. Small stakes that feel
enormous, a friendship that does the heavy lifting, read-aloud rhythm first.

## Main character

Nova, age nine, baker. Flour on her sleeves, checklist in her boot. She
inherited the bakery from her grandmother and is quietly sure everyone can
tell she is making it up as she goes.

## What they want

To bake festival buns so good that the whole crater square smells like her
grandmother's kitchen again.

## What they fear

That if one thing goes wrong — one bun, one delivery, one morning — everyone
will see she was never a real baker at all.

## Central conflict

The moon-sugar shipment is stuck on the far side of the Moon, the Star
Festival is tomorrow, and buns without moon-sugar don't glow. Nova's own
perfectionism is the second obstacle: she'd rather serve nothing than serve
something different.

## Setting

Crater Lane, a small settlement on the Moon: one bakery, one post depot, one
crater square strung with festival lanterns. The Moon Oven at the heart of
the bakery is older than anyone can explain.

## Tone

Warm, silly, snug. Trouble with a floor under it. Like a kitchen on a cold
night with the light on.

## Themes

- Good enough, shared, beats perfect and hoarded.
- Asking for help is a baker's skill too.

## Ending direction

Leans toward Nova inventing something new with what she has — and the crowd
loving it not because it's perfect, but because she made it. Not locked.

## Things this story should avoid

- No villains; the problem is a late shipment, not a bad person.
- No real danger to Nova or Crumb — worry, yes; peril, no.
- No stopping the story to announce the moral.

## Open questions

- What does moon-sugar actually taste like? (Nova would know. We don't, yet.)
- Does Grandmother appear in a memory, a recipe card, or not at all?
