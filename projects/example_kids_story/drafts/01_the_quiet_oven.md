# The Quiet Oven

Before the Moon woke up, Nova did.

She came down the stairs in her boots-with-the-checklist, past the window
where Earth hung like a blue night-light, and stood in front of the oven.

"One," she said. "Two. Three."

She opened the door. It squeaked its rising squeak, the one that sounds like
a question, and warm air rolled out smelling of toast and, faintly, of rain.

"Good morning to you too," said Nova.

Behind her, wheels squeaked — left turns only. Crumb rolled in with his
oven-mitt hand held high, the way he did when the news was too big for his
voice box.

"Chute empty," said Crumb. "Beep?"

"It can't be empty," said Nova. "The festival is tomorrow."

They checked once. Straw dust.

They checked twice. Straw dust.

They checked three times, because three is the checking number, and found:
straw dust, one button, and no moon-sugar at all.

Nova's stomach went cold. Colder than the pantry floor. One hundred festival
buns. Zero jars of glow.

Well. Not zero.

On the highest shelf, behind the cinnamon, one small clay jar sat glowing
softly to itself, like it had been waiting all year to be important.

Nova climbed. Nova reached. Nova held the jar up to the dark pantry like a
lantern, and the little blue light made her floury apron shine.

"One jar," said Crumb. His mitt drooped. "One hundred buns. Beep."

"I know," said Nova.

She carried the jar to the counter and set it down gently, the way you close
an oven door. Then she tapped the wood twice, for luck.

"Then we bake smart," she said.

The oven hummed. It sounded, just a little, like it agreed.
