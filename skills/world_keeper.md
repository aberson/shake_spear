# World Keeper

## Purpose

Organize worldbuilding so it serves the story instead of swallowing it. Capture the
locations, rules, and textures the scenes actually need, keep them findable in
`world/`, and resist the pull to build continents no scene will ever visit. Above
all: keep worldbuilding tied to scenes and character choices.

## Use when

- A scene introduces a place, object, institution, or rule worth its own file.
- The writer asks "how does this world work?" or "what should this place feel like?"
- A worldbuilding session (see `immersive_session.md`).
- World facts are scattered across drafts and need collecting into `world/`.

## Inputs to read first

- Existing files in `world/` â€” extend before duplicating.
- `story_bible.md` â€” setting, tone, and the themes the world should express.
- `continuity.md` â€” world rules and location facts already established.
- The files in `scenes/` that touch the element in question.

## Process

1. Start from what a scene needs, not from what the world could contain.
2. Track:
   - locations
   - objects
   - institutions
   - rules
   - sensory motifs
   - social norms
   - magic/science rules if relevant
   - what the reader needs to know now
   - what can remain implied
3. For each element that earns a file, use `../../templates/world_element.md` (from the story folder) and fill its
   headings: what it is, why it matters, sensory details, rules or constraints,
   connected characters, connected scenes, established facts, open questions.
4. Separate the two audiences: what the reader needs to know now goes into scenes;
   what can remain implied stays in the file as depth the prose can lean on.
5. Give every rule a cost or a limit â€” a rule nobody can break is scenery, not story.
6. After a scene, append newly established facts to the element's Established facts
   section rather than rewriting the file.
7. Flag contradictions between `world/` files and what a draft actually says; the
   writer decides which one changes.

## Output format

For a new element: a filled `world/<name>.md` following the template headings. For
an audit: a short list of proposed additions or contradictions, each pointing at the
scene that raised it. When answering "how does this work?": the rule, its cost or
limit, and one way it could complicate a scene the story already has.

## Things to avoid

- Overbuilding â€” encyclopedia entries for places, factions, and eras no scene visits.
- World rules that never cost a character anything.
- Dumping a world file into the draft as exposition; scenes get only what they need.
- Sensory motifs listed once and never threaded back into scenes.
- Letting the world dictate the plot. Keep worldbuilding tied to scenes and
  character choices.
