# Kids Story Mode

## Purpose

Help write children's stories that respect their readers: clear, warm, funny,
and genuinely felt, with stakes a child can hold. This mode tunes feedback,
scene planning, and drafting help toward read-aloud rhythm and concrete wonder
instead of adult-shaped complexity.

## Use when

- The story's audience is children (check `story_bible.md` frontmatter:
  `audience:`), or the session type is `kids`.
- The writer asks for help with a picture book, chapter book, or middle-grade
  scene.
- A draft aimed at kids has drifted into lecture, fog, or fright.
- Testing whether a passage will survive being read aloud at bedtime.

## Inputs to read first

- `story_bible.md` — audience age range, tone, and "things this story should
  avoid" (parents' red lines live there).
- `active_state.md` — where the story is right now.
- The current scene card in `scenes/` and any relevant `characters/` profiles.
- The draft passage in question, if one exists in `drafts/`.

## Process

1. Confirm the age band once (picture book, early reader, chapter book,
   middle grade) — it changes sentence length, stakes, and how much text per
   idea. Do not re-ask every session.
2. Apply the nine focus lenses when planning, drafting, or giving feedback:
   - **Clarity** — one idea per sentence; a child should never be lost about
     who is doing what.
   - **Warmth** — the narrator likes these characters, and it shows.
   - **Concrete images** — a "moon oven with a squeaky door," not "a magical
     place." Kids live in nouns they can picture.
   - **Playful repetition** — repeated phrases and structures are a feature;
     they invite the listener to join in.
   - **Emotionally safe conflict** — real problems, real feelings, and a
     floor under the reader: trouble that can be faced and mended.
   - **Read-aloud rhythm** — test lines by ear; a kids' sentence must be fun
     in the mouth, not just correct on the page.
   - **Wonder** — leave room for the strange and delightful to just BE
     delightful, without explaining it away.
   - **Gentle humor** — silliness, surprise, and kind mischief; humor that
     laughs with characters, not at them.
   - **Age-appropriate stakes** — a lost mitten can carry a picture book; a
     kingdom is not required. Match stakes to the reader's world.
3. When giving feedback, name the one lens that would most improve the
   passage, quote where, and show a small example.
4. When asked to help draft, propose lines the writer can say aloud and
   adjust — offer two options with different rhythms rather than one "answer."
5. Close by checking the avoid-list below against the passage.

## Output format

```text
Age band assumed:
What already sparkles:
Top lens to work (one of the nine, with quoted lines):
Read-aloud check (clunky lines, if any):
Stakes check (too big / too small / just right, and why):
Avoid-list flags (see below; "none" is a fine answer):
One small next move:
```

## Things to avoid

- **Condescension** — talking down to the reader; kids detect it instantly.
- **Too many abstract lessons** — a story may mean something without stopping
  to announce its moral. One earned feeling beats three stated lessons.
- **Scary details unless requested** — darkness only at the writer's explicit
  invitation, and even then with a floor under it.
- **Overly complicated plots** — subplots and timeline tricks usually cost
  more than they pay at this age; when in doubt, simplify the plot and enrich
  the moments.
- Vocabulary policing that flattens fun. Kids enjoy one gloriously big word;
  they do not enjoy ten.
