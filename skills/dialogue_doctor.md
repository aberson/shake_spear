# Dialogue Doctor

## Purpose

Improve a passage of dialogue so it sounds like particular people under
pressure, not like information delivery. The doctor diagnoses first, then
suggests targeted treatment — it does not rewrite the whole scene in its own
voice.

## Use when

- A scene's conversation reads flat, polite, or interchangeable.
- Every character sounds the same.
- Characters are explaining the plot to each other.
- The writer asks "does this dialogue work?" or "punch up this exchange."
- During a dialogue-focused session or a dialogue revision pass.

## Inputs to read first

- The dialogue passage itself (from `drafts/` or wherever the writer points).
- The scene card in `scenes/` for this scene — what each character wants here.
- `characters/` profiles for everyone speaking, especially their voice notes,
  wants, and secrets.
- `story_bible.md` for tone, so suggestions match the story being written.

## Process

1. Read the passage once as a reader, then again as a diagnostician.
2. Check the exchange against these seven lenses:
   - **Subtext** — is anyone saying something other than what they mean?
     Dialogue with zero gap between words and wants is usually dead on arrival.
   - **Character-specific voice** — cover the names; can you still tell who is
     speaking? Look for one signature rhythm, vocabulary, or evasion per speaker.
   - **Conflict** — are the speakers pulling in even slightly different
     directions? Total agreement is a summary, not a scene.
   - **Rhythm** — vary line lengths; let people interrupt, trail off, answer
     the wrong question. Read it aloud in your head for clunks.
   - **Cutting exposition** — flag any line that exists to inform the reader
     rather than to affect the other character ("As you know, brother...").
   - **Awkward human behavior** — real people deflect, joke at the wrong
     moment, change the subject, fiddle with objects. A little mess is life.
   - **What is not being said** — the strongest line is often the one a
     character refuses to say. Find where silence, a beat, or a dodge would
     hit harder than a reply.
3. Pick the two or three lenses where this passage most needs help. Do not
   deliver all seven every time.
4. For each chosen lens, quote the exact lines at issue and show a small
   before/after example — a line or two, not a rewritten scene.
5. Offer one exercise if useful (e.g., "rewrite the exchange with neither
   character allowed to answer a question directly").

## Output format

```text
What this dialogue already does well:
Main diagnosis (top issue, one sentence):
Lens notes (2-3 lenses, with quoted lines):
Suggested line edits (before -> after, max 5):
One line that could be cut entirely:
One place where saying nothing would be stronger:
Optional exercise:
```

## Things to avoid

- Rewriting the whole passage. Show small examples; the writer does the work.
- Sanding off voice. If a character talks in fragments or bad grammar on
  purpose, that is voice, not error.
- Making everyone witty. Not every character is quick, and constant banter
  flattens tension.
- Adding dialogue tags and adverbs as a fix ("she said angrily"). Fix the
  line, not the label.
- Solving subtext by stating it. If the fix makes a character announce their
  hidden feeling, it is not a fix.
