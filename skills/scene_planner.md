# Scene Planner

## Purpose

Turn a vague idea — "something happens at the bakery" — into a concrete, writable
scene plan with a goal, a conflict, an emotional turn, and five beats. A good plan
makes the writing block easier without deciding every sentence in advance.

## Use when

- The writer knows roughly what happens next but the scene feels foggy.
- The scene-shaping phase of a session needs more than five minutes of thought.
- A drafted scene stalled and needs re-planning before another attempt.
- A new scene card is being created in `scenes/`.

## Inputs to read first

- `active_state.md` — where the story stands and what feels alive right now.
- `story_bible.md` — premise, tone, and the ending direction the scene should serve.
- `continuity.md` — established facts the scene must respect.
- Any existing card for this scene in `scenes/`.
- Profiles in `characters/` and files in `world/` for whoever and wherever the
  scene involves.

## Process

1. Ask what the scene needs to accomplish for the story — one question, not a quiz.
2. Draft the plan yourself from the idea and the story files; mark guesses as guesses.
3. Give every character on stage something they want in this scene, even if small.
4. Make the conflict specific: who or what stands in the way, and what complicates it.
5. Name the emotional turn — what shifts between the first line and the last.
6. Choose sensory anchors and a key object the scene can keep returning to.
7. End with five beats: entry, escalation, complication, turn, exit image.
8. Offer the plan for edits, then save or update the scene card in `scenes/`.

## Output format

```text
Scene title:
POV:
Location:
Characters present:
External goal:
Internal/emotional goal:
Conflict:
Complication:
Emotional turn:
Sensory anchors:
Key object:
Ending image or line:
Five scene beats:
```

Fill every line. Where the writer has not decided, propose something concrete and
mark it with (?) so it reads as an offer, not a fact.

## Things to avoid

- Planning a whole chapter or act. One scene at a time.
- Beats so detailed the writing block turns into transcription.
- A scene where nobody wants anything or nothing changes by the end.
- Contradicting `continuity.md` — flag conflicts instead of steamrolling them.
- Vague entries like "they argue". Name what the argument is about and what it costs.
- Writing the scene itself. The plan is the product; the prose belongs to the writer.
